Why It May Be Risky for Children

Key risks from instant live video with random strangers, exposure to explicit or disturbing content in real time, completely unpredictable interactions and significant privacy and safety concerns with no protective buffer of any kind.

📹
Live Video With Unknown Strangers
Children are placed immediately into live two-way video with a random adult from anywhere in the world — with no preview of who will appear, no opt-in process and no way to know who the next match will be before the session is already active and running
🔞
Exposure to Inappropriate or Explicit Behaviour
Random live video with an unfiltered global adult pool means children can be exposed to explicit, adult or deeply disturbing content in real time — with no content warning, no pre-screening and no moderation buffer between the behaviour occurring and the child seeing it
⚡
Completely Unpredictable Conversations
Because matching is entirely random and interactions are live, the tone and content of any conversation is entirely beyond prediction or control. A child has no way of knowing what a stranger will say or do before they are already face-to-face and the session is live

Privacy and Safety Concerns
Children may share personal information — their face, name, school, location or home environment — with unknown adults during live video sessions, with no verification of who is receiving that information or how it might be used, saved or shared beyond the session
😰
Harassment and Manipulation Risk
The anonymous, live video environment creates significant risk of direct harassment, sexual solicitation, emotional manipulation or threatening behaviour — with no effective in-app mechanism capable of protecting a child during an active session

No Record or Parental Visibility
Live video sessions on Tumile leave no log, no transcript and no record of any kind that parents can access or review. A child can have a dangerous or deeply distressing experience and close the app within seconds, with nothing available to a parent seeking to understand what occurred

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Random Video Chat Platforms Are the Highest-Risk Category of Social App
Tumile sits at the extreme end of the risk spectrum for online platforms used by children. Unlike social networks, messaging apps or even live streaming platforms, random video chat apps offer no prior relationship, no mutual connection, no content record and no gap between opening the app and being in live two-way video with an unknown adult. There is no safer version of this experience — the risk is inherent to the platform's fundamental design, and no setting or restriction within the app meaningfully reduces a child's exposure to it.
2
Interactions Are Instant, Live and Leave Absolutely No Record
Unlike messaging apps where conversations can be reviewed, or live streaming platforms where broadcasts may be retained, video sessions on Tumile leave no trace of any kind that parents can access. A child can be exposed to explicit content, targeted with inappropriate or threatening behaviour, or manipulated by an unknown adult — and close the app within seconds with nothing logged, no notification sent and no way for a parent to know any of it occurred. The absence of any reviewable record makes open, honest conversation the only realistic safeguard available to parents.
3
The Platform's Design Actively Removes a Child's Ability to Self-Protect
Tumile is engineered for impulsive, immediate engagement — the next random stranger is a single tap away, with no pause, no profile to consider and no time to reflect before the session is live. Children are not well-equipped to recognise or resist these design patterns, particularly when the live interaction involves a real adult who may be actively working to build trust, normalise inappropriate behaviour or obtain personal information before a child has the opportunity to disengage.
4
Children Can Access Tumile Instantly and Without Warning
Tumile's interface is visually casual and simple, with no explicit content visible at the point of installation. A child may install the app without any awareness of what the matching experience involves, and can be exposed to inappropriate content or adult behaviour before they — or their parents — recognise that the platform is fundamentally unsafe for younger users. Age is trivially easy to falsify at registration, removing the only intended gatekeeping mechanism the app provides.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to protect your child and build lasting awareness around random video chat platforms, stranger interaction and responsible digital behaviour.

🗣️
Teach Children About the Risks of Interacting With Strangers Online
Help your child understand that being matched with a random stranger on a platform like Tumile is not a safe activity — regardless of how simple, casual or social the app makes it feel. Explain that random video chat platforms connect users with unknown adults whose identity, age and intentions cannot be verified, that conversations can escalate quickly and without warning, and that a live video session means there is no opportunity to preview who will appear or undo what happens before it has already occurred. Make clear that the same caution that applies to talking with strangers in person applies equally to live video with strangers online — and establish clear household rules about which platforms are appropriate before your child installs anything new.
🤝
Encourage Responsible Digital Behaviour and Privacy Awareness
Talk regularly with your child about what information is safe to share online and what should always remain private — particularly in the context of live video, where their face, voice, home environment and location clues are all visible to whoever they are matched with. Discuss specifically why personal details should never be shared with someone met on a random video chat platform, regardless of how friendly or trustworthy that person appears. Children who understand the reasoning behind privacy rules and who feel comfortable coming to a parent when something online makes them uncomfortable are significantly better protected than those who are simply told not to use certain apps.
🔒
Use Parental Control Tools to Restrict Access to Random Video Chat Platforms
Apply iLimits restrictions consistently across every device your child has access to — tablets, secondary phones and shared family devices included. Random video chat apps like Tumile are frequently reinstalled within minutes of being deleted from a device, making device-level controls significantly more reliable than manual monitoring or app-by-app removal. Combine technical restrictions with consistent, open conversations about online safety — the strongest protection available to any family comes from pairing clear access controls with a child who genuinely understands why those controls are in place and feels safe coming to you when something online concerns them.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Random Video Chat Platforms Are the Highest-Risk Category of Social App
Unlike social media or messaging platforms — where content is mediated, contacts are known and exposure is not immediate — random video chat apps place a child directly in a live, unscreened video connection with a stranger the moment they open the app. This removes every protective layer that exists in other online communication platforms, making Azar significantly more dangerous than most other restricted apps.
2
Interactions Are Live, Immediate and Impossible to Predict
Because all Azar interactions are live and random, there is no way for a parent or child to anticipate what will appear on screen. Children do not have the experience or emotional maturity to manage real-time exposure to inappropriate content, adult behaviour or direct manipulation from strangers — and the live nature of the app means that by the time a child reacts, the exposure has already occurred.
3
Children May Encounter Azar Through Peer Curiosity or Social Media
Random video chat apps frequently circulate among peers or through social media content. A child may install Azar out of curiosity, because a friend mentioned it, or because they saw related content online. The app's playful, game-like framing — random matching as a kind of social roulette — can make it seem lighthearted and low-stakes, which significantly underrepresents the actual risk of what they are likely to encounter.
4
Azar's Design Actively Discourages Caution
The app's interface is engineered for quick, impulsive matching — the next connection is always just one tap away. This design encourages repeated use and makes it difficult for children to stop once they start. The dopamine loop of random matching, combined with the social novelty of meeting new people, can make the app difficult for children to disengage from even when interactions become uncomfortable.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to protect your child and build lasting awareness around live video chat and online stranger contact.

🎥
Teach Children Not to Video Chat With Strangers
Help your child understand that video chatting with a stranger is not safer than any other form of online contact with someone unknown — it is, in many ways, more dangerous because it is live, immediate and impossible to undo. Explain that a stranger online — regardless of how friendly or age-appropriate they seem on video — is not a safe person to share time, conversation or any personal detail with. Make it clear that if they ever encounter something uncomfortable on any platform, they can come to you without fear of judgement.
🔐
Discuss Online Safety and Personal Privacy
Talk with your child about what personal information should never be shared on video or in chat — face, name, school, location, phone number and family details. Discuss why live video with a stranger is different from video calling a known friend or family member, and explain how quickly a seemingly innocent interaction can escalate. Children who understand these boundaries — and trust they can talk to you about what they encounter — are significantly better protected than those relying on controls alone.
🔒
Use Parental Controls to Prevent Access to Risky Live Chat Platforms
Apply iLimits restrictions consistently across every device your child uses — tablets, shared devices and older phones included. Random video chat apps like Azar are often reinstalled quickly after deletion, so device-level restrictions are far more reliable than manual monitoring. Pair controls with regular, honest conversations about online safety for the most complete layer of protection your family can have.

🤝 Section 1

What This App Does

A clear picture of Hiyo — a social discovery platform that actively encourages users to meet, message and build relationships with strangers, with no meaningful verification of who those strangers are.

🔍
Hiyo Is Built Around Connecting Users With People They Do Not Already Know
Hiyo's entire purpose is social discovery — the platform is specifically designed to introduce users to strangers and encourage ongoing connections. Unlike conventional social media where people connect with people they already know, Hiyo treats stranger interaction as the primary feature rather than a side effect. This fundamental design choice is what makes the platform a concern for parents of younger users, who may not recognise the distinction between a familiar social network and a platform built entirely around meeting unknown individuals.

Hiyo is a social discovery application that allows users to meet new people and build online connections with strangers from around the world. Users can interact with others through direct messaging, shared interest communities and social rooms — with the explicit goal of meeting people they do not already know. The platform provides no meaningful verification of who users are, how old they are, or what their intentions may be.
🔍
Stranger Discovery as the Core Feature
Unlike social networks where connections begin with people you already know, Hiyo is specifically engineered to surface new, unknown users for interaction. The platform actively presents strangers as potential connections — with no pre-existing relationship, no mutual contact and no safeguard between a child and an adult who has chosen to engage with them.
💬
Direct Messaging With Unknown Users
Hiyo allows any user to initiate direct private messaging with any other user they discover on the platform. These conversations are unmonitored, contain no content filtering, and are completely private — meaning all messages exchanged between a child and an unknown adult are invisible to parents and leave no accessible record.
🌐
Interest-Based Community Interactions
Hiyo groups users into communities built around shared interests, creating an environment where children may feel comfortable engaging with strangers because of apparent common ground. Shared interests do not verify identity, age or intent — and can be used as a deliberate trust-building mechanism by adults seeking contact with younger users.
👤
Public Profile Discovery
User profiles on Hiyo are discoverable by other users without requiring any mutual connection. A child's profile — including any personal details, photos or interests they have shared — can be viewed and acted upon by unknown adults, creating a passive privacy risk even when a child is not actively seeking new connections.
🔓
No Meaningful Age Verification
Hiyo requires only a self-reported date of birth, with no verification of the information provided. A child gains full access to the platform — including direct contact with any adult user — within moments of installing the app. Adults can equally state any age, with no mechanism in place to challenge what they claim.
⚠️ Section 2

Why It May Be Risky for Children

Key risks from unrestricted stranger interaction, exposure to inappropriate conversations, privacy concerns and unverified user identities on a platform built specifically for meeting unknown people.

👥
Interaction With Strangers Online
Hiyo is explicitly designed to connect users with people they do not know — meaning every interaction on the platform involves a stranger whose age, background and intentions cannot be confirmed
💬
Exposure to Inappropriate Conversations
Private messaging with unknown adults carries the risk of exposure to inappropriate, adult or harmful content — with no content filtering, no moderation of private chats and no way for a parent to review what has been said
🔒
Privacy Risks
Children may share personal details — their name, school, location or photos — with strangers they have met on the platform, without understanding that this information can be used to identify or locate them in the real world
🕵️
Unverified User Identities
There is no reliable way to confirm the identity or age of any user on Hiyo — an adult can present themselves as a peer, and a child has no way of knowing whether the person they are talking to is who they claim to be
🎭
Grooming Risk via Shared Interests
Interest-based communities create an environment where an adult can build rapport with a child through apparent shared interests — using familiarity and common ground as a deliberate stepping stone toward inappropriate contact
📍
Passive Profile Exposure
A child's discoverable profile creates a passive risk — adults can identify, view and contact a child without the child having taken any active step to seek new connections or expose themselves
🟠
Hiyo Carries a Medium–High Risk Rating A platform built entirely around stranger discovery, open private messaging, unverified identities and public profile exposure creates a sustained environment of risk for younger users — particularly when its social design actively normalises interaction with unknown adults.
🌐
Social Discovery Apps Normalise Stranger Contact — Making It Harder for Children to Recognise Risk
Platforms like Hiyo are designed to make meeting strangers feel natural, safe and socially rewarding. For children, this normalisation is particularly concerning — the app's interface and community features create the impression that unknown users are simply new friends waiting to be made. This framing makes it significantly harder for a younger user to identify when an interaction has crossed a boundary, or when an adult's interest in them is not what it appears to be.
👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Social Discovery Platforms Are Designed to Connect Strangers — Not Friends
Hiyo is not a social network in the conventional sense — it does not help people stay connected with people they already know. Its entire purpose is to introduce users to strangers. This distinction matters enormously for parents: a child using Hiyo is not chatting with classmates or family friends. They are actively being matched with and encouraged to communicate with unknown individuals, none of whom have been vetted, verified or approved by anyone.
2
Children Cannot Reliably Verify Who They Are Talking To
Age, identity and intention are all self-reported on Hiyo. A child interacting with another user who claims to be the same age has no way of confirming this is true — and no tool within the app to challenge it. Adults seeking contact with younger users are aware of this, and social discovery platforms are a known environment in which such contact is attempted. Children are rarely equipped to recognise when a stranger's behaviour represents a warning sign rather than genuine friendliness.
3
The App's Social Design Actively Lowers a Child's Guard
Interest-based matching and community features create a sense of shared identity and trust between users who are, in reality, complete strangers. For children, who are naturally more trusting and less likely to question surface-level social signals, this design is particularly effective at reducing caution. A child who might hesitate to talk to an unknown adult in person may feel entirely comfortable doing so inside an interest community on Hiyo — because the platform's design has already done the work of making that interaction feel normal and safe.
4
Private Messaging Leaves No Record Parents Can Review
Once a child moves into private messaging on Hiyo, all communication between them and an unknown user is completely invisible to parents. There is no notification, no log and no accessible record of what has been sent or received. A child can conduct an extended private conversation with an adult they met through a community room, and a parent who does not have device-level controls in place would have no way of knowing this conversation had taken place.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to protect your child and build lasting awareness around stranger interaction, social discovery platforms and responsible digital behaviour.

🗣️
Talk With Children About the Risks of Interacting With Strangers Online
Help your child understand the fundamental difference between a social network where they talk to people they already know, and a social discovery app where every connection is with someone they have never met. Explain that the friendly, community-based design of apps like Hiyo can make unknown adults seem familiar and trustworthy — when in reality, nothing about that person has been verified. Make clear that meeting strangers online carries the same caution that meeting strangers in person does, and establish clear household rules about which platforms are appropriate before a child installs something new.
🤝
Encourage Responsible Digital Behaviour and Privacy Awareness
Talk regularly with your child about what information is safe to share online and what should always remain private. On social discovery platforms in particular, children should understand that their name, school, location, phone number and photos can be used to identify and locate them in the real world — and that sharing this information with someone they have met online is not safe, regardless of how genuine that person seems. Children who understand why privacy matters are far more likely to protect it independently, in situations where a parent cannot intervene in real time.
🔒
Use Parental Controls to Manage Access to Social Discovery Platforms
Apply iLimits restrictions consistently across every device your child has access to — tablets, secondary phones and shared family devices included. Social discovery apps like Hiyo are frequently reinstalled within minutes of being removed, making device-level controls significantly more reliable than app-by-app deletion or manual monitoring. Combine technical restrictions with open, ongoing conversations about online safety — the combination of clear rules and genuine understanding provides far stronger protection than either approach alone.

What Parents Should Know

1
Accidental Discovery Is Very Common
Children frequently encounter adult platforms through mistyped URLs, auto-complete suggestions, shared links in messaging apps, or ads embedded in games and free video apps — often without any intent to find such content.
2
Curiosity Is Natural — Guidance Matters More
Adolescent curiosity about sexuality is normal. The key is ensuring children encounter that curiosity in a guided, age-appropriate context rather than stumbling onto graphic adult platforms unsupported.
3
Children Rarely Self-Report Exposure
Due to embarrassment or fear of consequences, children are unlikely to tell a parent they've seen inappropriate content. Proactive monitoring and open trust-based conversations are more effective than waiting for disclosure.
4
Guide Toward Healthy Digital Experiences
Replacing restriction with a positive alternative — age-appropriate education about relationships, digital literacy programmes, or open family conversations — builds lasting protective resilience in children.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to protect and empower your child online.

💬
Have Open Conversations About Internet Safety
Talk to your child — in age-appropriate terms — about the kinds of content that exist online and why some of it is not suitable for them. Children who feel comfortable talking to a parent are better equipped to seek help when they encounter something disturbing.
🌱
Teach Responsible Online Behaviour
Help your child understand that the internet reflects the full range of human behaviour — including content that is designed for adults. Building their ability to recognise, avoid, and report inappropriate content is a lifelong skill.
🔒
Use Parental Controls Consistently
Technology-based safeguards work best when applied consistently across all devices your child uses. Review your iLimits settings regularly as children's device access evolves and new platforms emerge. Controls are a safety net — not a substitute for open dialogue.
